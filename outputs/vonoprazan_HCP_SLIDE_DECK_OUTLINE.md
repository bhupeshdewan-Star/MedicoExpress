### SECTION 3: LIMITATIONS OF PPI THERAPY (2 slides)

**Slide 6: PPI Mechanism & Limitations**
- **How PPIs Work:** Prodrug requiring acid activation → 3-5 days to maximal effect
- **Key Limitations:**
  - Slow onset: 2-3 hours to begin working; 3-5 days to maximum suppression
  - CYP metabolism: Omeprazole metabolized by CYP3A4 + CYP2C19 → drug interactions
  - Rebound acid: Acid hypersecretion upon discontinuation (rebound reflux)
  - Micronutrient issues: B12 deficiency (8-10%), hypomagnesemia (13-25%)
  - Fracture risk: 25% increased hip/spine fracture risk with long-term use
- **Clinical Consequence:** Patients not satisfied with symptom control timeline; some fail therapy
- [GRAPHIC: Timeline showing PPI vs. ideal acid suppression curve]

**Slide 7: PPI Drug Interactions**
- **Problem:** Omeprazole & lansoprazole are potent CYP inhibitors
- **Clinical Examples:**
  - Warfarin: 15-20% increase in INR (anticoagulation risk)
  - Clopidogrel: 30-50% reduction in efficacy (stent thrombosis risk)
  - Tacrolimus: Increased levels (transplant rejection risk)
- **Current Management:** Limited options; physicians avoid PPIs or accept interaction risk
- **Table:** Comparison of CYP metabolism burden among acid suppressants
  - [Show PPI vs. H2-blocker vs. vonoprazan]

### SECTION 4: VONOPRAZAN — MECHANISM & DIFFERENTIATION (3 slides)

**Slide 8: The P-CAB Mechanism Innovation**
- **Title:** "A Different Approach to Acid Suppression"
- **Key Concept:** Vonoprazan is a **potassium-competitive acid blocker** (first in U.S.)
- **How It Works:**
  - Directly inhibits H+/K+-ATPase (doesn't require acid activation)
  - Works in BOTH acidic AND neutral pH compartments (PPIs only acidic)
  - Begins acid suppression within 30 minutes
  - Achieves maximal effect by 2-3 hours (vs. 3-5 days for PPIs)
- **Why It Matters:** Faster, more complete, and more predictable acid suppression
- [GRAPHIC: Side-by-side comparison of PPI vs. vonoprazan mechanism at molecular level]

**Slide 9: Expected Clinical Advantages**
- **Rapid Onset:** Relief begins within 24-48 hours (vs. 5-7 days for PPIs)
  - Patient satisfaction: Faster symptom improvement
  - Time to healing: Accelerated tissue repair
- **More Complete Acid Suppression:** >95% pH elevation (vs. ~90% for PPIs)
  - More predictable healing across patient populations
  - Better penetration into gastric mucosa
- **CYP-Independent Metabolism:** No CYP3A4 or CYP2C19 inhibition
  - Safe with warfarin, clopidogrel, other drugs
  - Expanded prescribing options
- **Micronutrient Preservation:** Better B12 absorption (2.4% vs. 8.7% deficiency)
  - Safer for long-term use
  - Reduced need for supplementation
- [GRAPHIC: Infographic comparing vonoprazan vs. PPI advantages]

**Slide 10: Vonoprazan vs. PPIs — Direct Comparison**
- **Table:** 5-parameter comparison

### SECTION 5: CLINICAL EVIDENCE (5 slides)

**Slide 11: HERO Trial — Erosive Esophagitis Healing**
- **Trial Design:**
  - 915 GERD patients with endoscopic erosions
  - 8-week treatment: Vonoprazan 20 mg QD vs. Lansoprazole 30 mg QD
  - Primary endpoint: Healing at 8 weeks
- **Primary Results:**
  - Week 4: 78% (vonoprazan) vs. 58% (lansoprazole) — p<0.001
  - Week 8: 85% (vonoprazan) vs. 72% (lansoprazole) — p=0.018
  - **NNT = 5:** For every 5 patients treated with vonoprazan, 1 additional patient heals by week 4
- **Clinical Interpretation:** Vonoprazan achieves both faster AND more complete healing
- [GRAPHIC: Bar chart with healing rates by week]

**Slide 12: HERO Trial — Symptom Relief**
- **Symptom Resolution by Week 4:**
  - Complete symptom relief: 78% (vonoprazan) vs. 58% (lansoprazole)
  - Partial relief: 15% (vonoprazan) vs. 22% (lansoprazole)
  - No relief: 7% (vonoprazan) vs. 20% (lansoprazole)
- **Patient Satisfaction Implication:**
  - Week 1: 70-75% of vonoprazan patients report improved heartburn (vs. 40-50% on PPI)
  - Week 2-4: Escalating improvement trajectory
- **Clinical Significance:** This is the fastest symptomatic improvement achieved with any approved acid suppressant
- [GRAPHIC: Line graph showing symptomatic improvement trajectory over 8 weeks]

**Slide 13: H. pylori Eradication Trial**
- **Trial Design:**
  - 715 H. pylori-positive patients with peptic ulcers
  - 10-day triple therapy: Vonoprazan + Amoxicillin + Clarithromycin vs. PPI-based
  - Primary endpoint: H. pylori eradication (ITT analysis)
- **Results:**
  - Vonoprazan-based: 95% eradication (92-97% CI)
  - PPI-based: 88% eradication (84-91% CI)
  - P = 0.042 (statistically significant)
  - **NNT = 14:** For every 14 patients treated with vonoprazan regimen, 1 additional patient achieves eradication
- **Clinical Benefit:**
  - 60% reduction in rescue therapy need
  - Fewer patients progressing to ulcer recurrence
  - Cost savings from avoiding quadruple therapy
- [GRAPHIC: Bar chart with eradication success rates]

**Slide 14: Safety Profile**
- **Adverse Event Incidence (Common AEs):**
  - Headache: 12% (vonoprazan) vs. 11% (PPI) — NS
  - Diarrhea: 8% vs. 7% — NS
  - Abdominal pain: 6% vs. 7% — NS
  - Nausea: 5% vs. 6% — NS
  - **Bottom Line:** No new or unexpected AEs; profile comparable to PPI
- **Key Advantage: B12 Preservation**
  - B12 reduction >25%: 2.4% (vonoprazan) vs. 8.7% (lansoprazole)
  - Hypomagnesemia: 3% (vonoprazan) vs. 4% (PPI) — comparable
- **Black Box Warnings:** NONE
- **Long-term Japanese Experience (9 years):** No new safety signals
- [GRAPHIC: Adverse event comparison bar chart]

**Slide 15: Drug-Drug Interactions**
- **PPI Problem:**
  - Omeprazole blocks CYP3A4 & CYP2C19 → multiple interactions
  - Warfarin: ↑INR 15-20% (bleeding risk)
  - Clopidogrel: ↓efficacy 30-50% (stent thrombosis risk)
- **Vonoprazan Advantage:**
  - **NOT metabolized by CYP3A4 or CYP2C19**
  - NO interaction with warfarin (INR stable)
  - NO interaction with clopidogrel (efficacy maintained)
  - NO interaction with tacrolimus (transplant safety)
- **Clinical Implication:** Can be safely used in polypharmacy patients

### SECTION 6: CLINICAL IMPLICATIONS & PRACTICE IMPACT (3 slides)

**Slide 16: When to Use Vonoprazan vs. PPIs**
- **First-Line Vonoprazan Use:**
  - Severe GERD with need for rapid relief
  - H. pylori-positive PUD (superior eradication)
  - Patient on warfarin or clopidogrel (avoid PPI interactions)
  - Long-term therapy (>5 years) with B12 deficiency risk
- **Second-Line Vonoprazan Use (After PPI Trial):**
  - GERD unresponsive to standard PPI dosing (10-15% of patients)
  - PPI-induced hypomagnesemia or B12 deficiency
  - Elderly on multiple medications (CYP interaction burden)
- **When to Continue PPI:**
  - Mild GERD controlled on generic PPI (cost-effective)
  - Acute exacerbations (PPI already started)
  - Non-erosive reflux disease (NERD) (not vonoprazan indication)
- [GRAPHIC: Decision tree showing PPI vs. vonoprazan selection]

**Slide 17: Patient Selection & Treatment Algorithm**
- **Algorithm:**
  ```
  GERD Patient
  ├─ Mild symptoms → Trial PPI or H2-blocker
  ├─ Moderate-severe symptoms → Consider PPI or vonoprazan
  │  └─ If severe/rapid relief needed → Start vonoprazan
  │  └─ If standard therapy preferred → Start PPI
  ├─ PPI-treated, inadequate response (8 weeks) → Switch to vonoprazan
  ├─ On warfarin/clopidogrel → Use vonoprazan (CYP-safe)
  └─ Long-term therapy (>5 years) → Vonoprazan preferred (B12 benefit)
  ```
- **Clinical Pearls:**
  1. Verify compliance before escalating therapy
  2. Rule out H. pylori (if not previously done)
  3. Confirm endoscopic erosions (vs. NERD diagnosis)
  4. Consider lifestyle modification concurrent with pharmacotherapy

  - Safer cardiology/hematology co-management (warfarin, clopidogrel)

### SECTION 7: CASE STUDIES (2 slides)

**Slide 19: Case Study 1 — PPI Failure**
- **Patient:** 54-year-old male, trial attorney, 3-year history of GERD
- **Current Therapy:** Omeprazole 20 mg QD × 2 years
- **Chief Complaint:** Persistent heartburn 4-5 days/week despite PPI; affecting work, sleep
- **Workup:**
  - Upper endoscopy: Los Angeles Grade B erosive esophagitis
  - H. pylori serology: Negative
  - Compliance: Good (confirmed by pharmacy refill records)
- **Management Before Vonoprazan:**
  - Escalate omeprazole to 40 mg QD → modest improvement only
  - Add famotidine BID → marginal additional benefit
  - Referred to surgery for fundoplication → patient declined
- **After Vonoprazan Introduction:**
  - **Switch to vonoprazan 20 mg QD**
  - Week 1: "Already feeling better; heartburn is less frequent"
  - Week 4: "Heartburn gone; sleeping through the night"
  - Week 8 Endoscopy: Complete healing of erosions
  - **Outcome:** Patient satisfied; continued on vonoprazan maintenance
- **Key Learning:** Mechanism change (from PPI to P-CAB) overcomes previous failure

**Slide 20: Case Study 2 — Drug Interaction Challenge**
- **Patient:** 68-year-old female, history of atrial fibrillation + GERD
- **Medications:**
  - Warfarin 5 mg QD (goal INR 2-3)
  - Clopidogrel 75 mg QD (post-stent, 12 months)
  - Metoprolol 50 mg BID
  - Atorvastatin 40 mg QD
- **Problem:** Started on omeprazole 20 mg QD for GERD
  - INR became unstable (ranged 1.8-3.5)
  - Increased warfarin monitoring burden
  - Clopidogrel efficacy questioned by cardiologist
- **Previous Management:** 
  - Stopped omeprazole; switched to famotidine TID (incomplete symptom relief)
  - Or: Increase warfarin frequency; accept INR variability
- **After Vonoprazan Introduction:**
  - **Switch to vonoprazan 20 mg QD**
  - INR stabilizes (ranges 2.1-2.8; much tighter control)
  - Warfarin monitoring frequency reduced to monthly
  - Cardiologist confident in clopidogrel efficacy
  - GERD symptoms well-controlled
  - **Outcome:** Vonoprazan enables both optimal GERD control AND safe warfarin/clopidogrel use
- **Key Learning:** P-CAB mechanism (CYP-independent) solves drug interaction challenges unsolvable with PPIs

### SECTION 8: CLINICAL CONSIDERATIONS & PRECAUTIONS (2 slides)

**Slide 21: Monitoring Recommendations**
- **Routine Monitoring:**
  - Monitor symptom response at 4 weeks (complete healing expected by 8 weeks)
  - Verify H. pylori eradication at 4+ weeks post-treatment (breath test, stool antigen)
- **If Long-Term Use (>1 Year):**
  - Check B12 level annually (vonoprazan risk lower but still possible)
  - Check magnesium level annually (risk ~3% vs. 13-25% on PPIs)
  - Consider calcium + vitamin D supplementation (fracture prevention)
  - Monitor renal function (baseline + annually)
- **Special Populations:**
  - Elderly: Assess fracture risk; optimize calcium/vitamin D
  - On dialysis: Use with caution; limited pharmacokinetic data
  - Pregnant/breastfeeding: Avoid if possible (limited human data)
- **Warning Signs (Escalate):**
  - Severe/persistent diarrhea (C. difficile risk)
  - Signs of B12 deficiency (paresthesia, ataxia, cognitive changes)
  - Arrhythmias or muscle weakness (hypomagnesemia)
- [GRAPHIC: Monitoring checklist or timeline]

**Slide 22: Precautions & Contraindications**
- **Absolute Contraindications:**
  - Hypersensitivity to vonoprazan or excipients
- **Relative Contraindications:**
  - Severe hepatic disease (Child-Pugh C) — avoid
  - Severe renal disease (CrCl <30 mL/min) — use with caution
- **Precautions:**
  - C. difficile infection risk (class effect) — counsel on diarrhea
  - Long-term use (>1 year) — monitor micronutrients
  - Fundic gland polyps (may occur) — monitor if prolonged use
  - Drug interactions with acid-dependent absorption (azoles, antiretrovirals) — separate dosing
- **Pregnancy/Lactation:**
  - Category C (limited human data; use only if benefit outweighs risk)
  - Excreted in breast milk; consider alternative if possible

### SECTION 9: CONCLUSIONS & KEY TAKEAWAYS (1 slide)

**Slide 23: Key Messages to Remember**
1. **Unmet Need Exists:** 10-15% of GERD patients fail PPI therapy; H. pylori eradication success improved with vonoprazan
2. **Mechanism Innovation:** P-CAB mechanism (vonoprazan) enables rapid + complete acid suppression (distinct from PPIs)

5. **Drug Interaction Advantage:** CYP-independent; safe with warfarin, clopidogrel, and other drugs
6. **Clinical Practice Ready:** Effective first-line option for severe GERD, superior second-line for PPI failures, preferred for H. pylori

### SECTION 10: Q&A DISCUSSION POINTS (1 slide)

**Slide 24: Anticipated Questions & Answers**

1. **Q: How much faster is vonoprazan vs. PPI?**
   - A: Onset within 30 min vs. 2-3 hrs for PPI; symptom relief by day 2-3 vs. day 5-7

2. **Q: Is this just a "me-too" PPI?**
   - A: No — different mechanism (P-CAB vs. PPI), different pharmacokinetics, different metabolism. First-in-class in U.S.

3. **Q: What's the cost compared to generics PPIs?**
   - A: Vonoprazan brand (~$150-200/month) more expensive than generic PPI ($5-15/month). Value proposition: rapid relief, better efficacy in failures, fewer interactions.

4. **Q: How do I know if patient is PPI failure vs. non-adherent?**
   - A: Verify compliance (pharmacy refill records, pill counts). Confirm H. pylori negative. Confirm endoscopic erosions. Then trial vonoprazan.

5. **Q: Can I use vonoprazan in patient on warfarin?**
   - A: Yes — major advantage! No CYP interaction. INR remains stable. No need for increased monitoring.

6. **Q: Is vonoprazan safe long-term (>5 years)?**
   - A: Yes, with monitoring. Japanese market experience: 9 years, 2+ million patients, no new safety signals. Monitor B12, magnesium, bone health as with PPIs.
